Q

What is alloy grade?

A

Alloy grade is a numbering system used to distinguish between different alloys. Since different alloys have different chemical compositions and varying properties, they must be named to differentiate them. For example, for an alloy containing 72% nickel and 14% chromium, the grade is Inconel 600. Of course, the same grade may have multiple designations. For example, UNS N10276 and W.Nr. 2.4819 are alternative names for Hastelloy C-276, but they all refer to the same grade.

Q

Are nickel alloys and superalloys the same?

A

The definitions of nickel alloys and superalloys are not the same. Nickel alloy refers to an alloy where nickel is the primary element, while superalloy refers to an alloy used at high temperatures. However, there is a high degree of overlap between them, though there are exceptions. For example, Monel 400 is a nickel alloy but is not a superalloy. Conversely, among superalloys, in addition to nickel alloys, there are also cobalt alloys and other alloys dominated by different elements.

Q

What is the difference between Monel, Inconel, Incoloy, and Hastelloy?

A

Monel, Inconel, Incoloy, and Hastelloy all represent groups of alloys. Monel is a nickel-copper alloy and serves as a generic term for a series of such alloy grades. For example, Monel 400 and Monel K-500 both belong to the Monel family, but they differ in their nickel and copper content, as well as the proportions of other trace elements. Similarly, Inconel refers to nickel-chromium alloys or nickel-chromium-iron alloys. Incoloy refers to nickel-iron-chromium alloys or iron-nickel-chromium alloys. Hastelloy refers to nickel-chromium-molybdenum alloys or nickel-molybdenum alloys. The element listed first in the name represents the highest percentage in the alloy.
